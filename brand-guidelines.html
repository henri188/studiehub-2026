§ 01 Identity

The mark is a droplet. Whole, weighted, never sharp.

One form, no ornament.

The droplet is the entire identity. It carries surface tension at its base and rises to a single point — the visual signature of a substance that wants to be whole. Use it as the primary mark across every surface.

Avoid: outlining, rotating off-axis, adding gradients, recoloring outside the system palette.

Clear space

Minimum margin equal to the width of the mark's widest point — half the mark's diameter — on every side. Never crowd.

§ 03 Typography

Two voices. One precise, one literary.

Display · Headlines · UI Hanken Grotesk 300 · 400 · 500 · 700
Quiet matter, at rest.

A neo-grotesk with a soft heart. Tight tracking, geometric numerals, used for everything structural.

Body · Long-form · Italic emphasis Newsreader 300 · 400 · 500 — Optical sizes

A liquid metal pools where it is welcomed. It does not announce itself — it is recognized by weight, by silver edge, by the way it gathers light. The typeface that names this work should do the same: warm, literate, generous in italic, never showy.

Humanist serif. Used for body copy, italic flourish in display lockups, and any text meant to be read slowly.

Memorandum — 05 · 2026

On the weight of quiet things.

Field note · Vol. I, No. 003
Subject

A defense of restraint as a positive quality, not the absence of one.

Length

Two pages, approx. 740 words.

For

The personal record.

Mercury is unusual among the metals — it is liquid at the temperatures we live in. This makes it useful for instruments and dangerous for everything else. The name we have for it in Greek, hydrárgyros, simply means water-silver, which is exactly what it looks like in the palm of a hand.

What follows is a short argument for taking that quality — the willingness to hold its own shape without insisting on it — as a posture for the small body of work collected under this mark.

I. Weight, not loudness.

A drop of mercury weighs more than its size suggests. The same principle applies to writing, to objects, and to the surfaces they sit on. A page should feel heavier than it looks. A sentence should carry more than it announces.

II. The room around the thing.

The Bauhaus understood this, and Braun later. So did the Japanese print designers who left a quarter of a page empty and called it composition. The silence is the work.
